üî¨ What Are Research Methods?

Research methods are systematic approaches used to collect, analyze, and interpret data to answer research questions.

The Three Main Approaches

Method Focus Data Type
Qualitative Understanding experiences, meanings, and perspectives Words, images, observations
Quantitative Measuring variables and testing hypotheses Numbers, statistics
Mixed-Methods Combining both approaches Both words and numbers
Remember: No method is inherently better than another‚Äîeach serves different research purposes!

üó£Ô∏è Qualitative Research

What Is It?

Qualitative research explores complex human experiences, behaviors, and social phenomena through in-depth, descriptive data.

Key Characteristics

  • Focuses on "why" and "how" questions
  • Uses small, purposeful samples
  • Collects detailed, rich data
  • Researcher is actively involved in data collection
  • Findings are interpretive and context-specific

Common Data Collection Methods

  • Interviews: One-on-one conversations
  • Focus Groups: Group discussions
  • Observations: Watching and recording behaviors
  • Document Analysis: Examining texts, images, or artifacts

üó£Ô∏è Qualitative Research Example

Research Question:

"How do families of teenage suicide victims cope with their loss?"

Method:

Researchers conducted unstructured interviews with families of 10 teenage suicide victims in rural Sweden.

Data Collection:

  • Interviews began with: "Tell us about your loved one"
  • Families could discuss whatever felt important
  • Researchers listened and followed up on themes

Key Finding:

Even as life returned to "normal," families continued struggling with the question of why the suicide occurred‚Äîespecially when it was unexpected.

Why Qualitative?

This approach captured the lived experience and emotional complexity that numbers alone couldn't reveal.

üìä Quantitative Research

What Is It?

Quantitative research uses numerical data and statistical analysis to test hypotheses and measure relationships between variables.

Key Characteristics

  • Focuses on "how many" and "to what extent" questions
  • Uses large, representative samples
  • Collects measurable, numerical data
  • Researcher maintains objectivity
  • Results can be generalized to larger populations

Common Data Collection Methods

  • Surveys/Questionnaires: Standardized questions
  • Experiments: Controlled tests
  • Tests: Standardized assessments
  • Secondary Data Analysis: Using existing datasets

üìä Quantitative Research Example

Research Question:

"What is the relationship between feelings of loneliness and anxiety symptoms?"

Method:

  • Administered Lonely Inventory scale
  • Measured anxiety through standardized questionnaire
  • Tested 500 participants
  • Used statistical analysis to find correlations

Results:

Statistical analysis showed a significant positive correlation between loneliness scores and anxiety levels.

Why Quantitative?

This approach allowed researchers to:

  • Measure the strength of the relationship
  • Test the finding across a large sample
  • Make generalizable conclusions

üé≤ Sampling Methods

Sampling is the process of selecting participants from a larger population to study.

Simple Random Sampling

Every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected.

Example: Drawing names from a hat, or using a random number generator

When to Use:

  • You want results that can be generalized to the whole population
  • All members of the population are accessible
  • You want to minimize bias

Limitations:

  • If sampling online, people without internet access cannot be selected
  • May not be practical for hard-to-reach populations
üí° Remember: The goal of random sampling is to get a representative sample that reflects the larger population.

üîÑ Mixed-Methods Research

What Is It?

Mixed-methods research combines both qualitative and quantitative approaches to provide a more complete understanding of a research problem.

Why Use Mixed-Methods?

  • Triangulation: Validate findings using multiple sources
  • Complementarity: Quantitative data shows "what" and "how much," while qualitative explains "why" and "how"
  • Development: Use one method to inform the other
  • Expansion: Explore different aspects of the research question

Example Study:

Research Question: "How does ethnicity relate to female engineering students' sense of belonging?"

Phase 1 (Quantitative): Survey students rating their sense of belonging‚Äîfound no statistical differences between ethnic groups.

Phase 2 (Qualitative): Interviews revealed that minority students valued the university's cultural diversity, which enhanced their sense of belonging.

Insight: Without the qualitative phase, researchers would have missed this important finding!

ü§î How to Choose the Right Method

Ask Yourself These Questions

Question Leads To
Do I want to understand experiences and meanings? ‚Üí Qualitative
Do I want to measure relationships between variables? ‚Üí Quantitative
Do I need both depth and breadth? ‚Üí Mixed-Methods
Is my question exploratory ("What is happening?") ‚Üí Qualitative
Is my question confirmatory ("Does X cause Y?") ‚Üí Quantitative
Important: Your research question should guide your methodology‚Äînot the other way around!

üìß Writing Professional Emails

Why Does This Matter?

As a researcher, you'll need to communicate with advisors, collaborators, participants, and funding bodies. Professional emails demonstrate your credibility and respect for others' time.

Email Structure

  1. Subject Line: Clear and specific
  2. Greeting: Professional salutation
  3. Opening: State your purpose immediately
  4. Body: Provide necessary details concisely
  5. Call to Action: Clearly state what you need
  6. Closing: Thank the recipient
  7. Signature: Include your full contact information

üìß Professional Email Example

Subject: Request for Feedback on Research Proposal - Mixed-Methods Study

Dear Dr. Martinez,

I hope this email finds you well. I am writing to seek your guidance on a research proposal I am developing for my English Composition course at University of the People.

My study explores how social media usage affects college students' writing skills. I plan to use a mixed-methods approach, combining:

  • Quantitative surveys measuring time spent on social media and writing assessment scores (200 participants)
  • Qualitative interviews exploring students' perceptions of how social media influences their writing (15 participants)

I believe this methodology is appropriate because the quantitative data will establish whether a correlation exists, while the qualitative component will help explain the mechanisms behind any observed patterns.

Would you be available for a brief 15-minute call next week to discuss my approach? I would greatly appreciate your insights, particularly regarding my sampling strategy.

Thank you for considering my request. I look forward to hearing from you.

Best regards,
Sarah Johnson
Student, ENGL 1102
sarah.johnson@uopeople.edu

‚ùì Test Your Understanding

Question 1: A researcher wants to understand "how many students drop out of college annually?" Which research method should they use?
Question 2: A researcher wants to explore "why students feel disconnected from their college community." Which research method is most appropriate?
Question 3: What are the three main components that make an email professional?

‚úîÔ∏è Answers

Answer 1: Quantitative research. This question asks "how many," which requires numerical data and statistical analysis to measure dropout rates.
Answer 2: Qualitative research. The word "why" indicates a need to understand experiences, meanings, and perspectives‚Äîbest captured through interviews, focus groups, or observations.
Answer 3: 1) Clarity - clear purpose and request; 2) Professional tone - formal language and respectful approach; 3) Conciseness - brief and to-the-point, respecting the recipient's time.
Answer 4: Use mixed-methods when you need both depth (qualitative) and breadth (quantitative), when findings from one method can explain or validate the other, or when your research question requires both numerical data and contextual understanding.
Answer 5: Every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected, which minimizes bias and helps create a representative sample.
