Understanding Problem Statements

What is a Problem Statement?

A problem statement is a clear, concise description of an issue or gap in knowledge that your research aims to address.

Key Components:

  • Specific: Clearly defined scope and focus
  • Evidence-based: Supported by credible research
  • Real-life issue: Addresses actual problems, not just academic gaps
  • Significant: Has meaningful consequences if not addressed

Example Problem Statement:

"Only 6% of CEOs in Fortune 500 companies are women (Center for Leadership Studies, 2019)."

This statement is specific, evidence-based, addresses a real-world issue, and has significant implications for gender equality in leadership.

Crafting Your Problem Statement

Essential Elements

Three-Part Structure:

  1. Context & Problem: What is the issue?
  2. Consequences: What happens if we don't solve it?
  3. Gap: What knowledge is missing?

Writing Tips:

  • Start with: "The problem to be addressed through this study is..."
  • Use recent citations (within last 5 years)
  • Be specific about who is affected
  • Keep it focused and manageable
  • 250-300 words maximum

Poor Example:

"People don't like their jobs" - Too vague, no evidence

Better Example:

"Employee well-being among mid-level managers in financial services has declined, with 50% reporting dissatisfaction with key workplace aspects (Starkman, 2015), leading to 18.6% annual turnover rates..."

Research Objectives

Defining Your Research Goals

Research objectives describe what your research is trying to achieve and explain why you are pursuing it.

Key Functions:

  • Establish scope and depth of your project
  • Contribute to research design - guide methodology choices
  • Indicate contribution to existing knowledge

Placement in Your Paper:

Objectives should appear in the introduction of your research paper, at the end of your problem statement.

Example Research Aim vs. Objectives:

Aim: To examine contributory factors to muscle retention in elderly people.

Objectives:

  • To assess the relationship between sedentary habits and muscle atrophy
  • To determine the impact of dietary factors on muscular health
  • To determine the effect of physical activity on participants' muscular health

SMART Research Objectives

Making Your Objectives Effective

S

Specific

M

Measurable

A

Achievable

R

Realistic

T

Timely

SMART Criteria Explained:

  • Specific: Well-defined, clear, and unambiguous
  • Measurable: With criteria that measure progress toward goal accomplishment
  • Achievable: Attainable and not impossible to achieve
  • Realistic: Within reach and relevant to your life purpose
  • Timely: With a clearly defined timeline including a starting date and target date

Example: "By August 1, I will obtain a gym membership at my local community center and work out four days a week. Every week, I will aim to lose one pound of body fat."

Using Bloom's Taxonomy

Choosing the Right Action Verbs

Remember

cite, define, describe, identify, list, recall

Understand

classify, compare, explain, illustrate, interpret

Apply

apply, calculate, demonstrate, execute, implement

Analyze

analyze, break down, categorize, compare, contrast

Evaluate

appraise, argue, assess, conclude, critique

Create

arrange, assemble, build, create, design, develop

Example Objectives Using Bloom's Verbs:

  • "To analyze the relationship between..."
  • "To evaluate the effectiveness of..."
  • "To compare the outcomes between..."
  • "To assess the impact of..."

Developing Research Questions

From Objectives to Specific Questions

Research questions are specific inquiries that help achieve your objectives. They should be clear, focused, and answerable through research.

Techniques for Generating Questions:

  • Literature Review: Look at "future research" suggestions in recent articles
  • Behavioral Analysis: Ask about causes, effects, frequency, or intensity
  • Relationship Questions: Explore connections between variables
  • Comparative Questions: Compare different groups or situations

Key Questions to Ask Yourself:

  • What are some possible causes of the behavior?
  • What are some possible effects of the behavior?
  • What types of individuals might exhibit more or less of the behavior?
  • Does the behavior occur more or less in certain situations?

Evaluating Research Questions

Two Key Criteria

1. Interestingness

  • Answer is in doubt: There should be reasonable chance for different answers
  • Fills a gap: Addresses something not already answered
  • Practical implications: Has real-world relevance and application

2. Feasibility

  • Time constraints: Can be completed within available timeframe
  • Resource availability: You have access to necessary materials and participants
  • Technical knowledge: Within your skill level or learnable
  • Ethical considerations: Can be conducted ethically

Example Assessment:

Question: "Does note-taking by hand improve learning compared to digital note-taking?"

Interesting? ✓ Practical implications for education policy

Feasible? ✓ Can be tested with student participants

Week 4 Assignment

Crafting Your Problem Statement & Objectives

Assignment Requirements:

  • Word Count: 500-750 words (excluding references and title)
  • Format: Double-spaced, Times New Roman, 12pt font
  • Sources: Use high-quality, credible, relevant sources
  • Citations: Proper in-text citations and reference list

Structure:

  1. Problem Statement: Describe the issue/gap and justify its importance
  2. Research Objectives: Define overall goals that address the problem
  3. Research Questions: Frame specific inquiries linked to objectives

Success Tips:

  • Build on your literature review from previous weeks
  • Use SMART criteria for objectives
  • Choose appropriate Bloom's taxonomy verbs
  • Ensure questions are both interesting and feasible

Writing Guide

Step-by-Step Process

Step 1: Problem Statement (150-200 words)

  1. Start with: "The problem to be addressed through this study is..."
  2. Describe the specific issue with statistical evidence
  3. Explain consequences of NOT solving the problem
  4. Identify the knowledge gap your research will address

Step 2: Research Objectives (100-150 words)

  • Write 2-4 SMART objectives
  • Use appropriate Bloom's taxonomy verbs
  • Ensure they directly address your problem statement
  • Make them specific and measurable

Step 3: Research Questions (200-250 words)

  • Develop 3-5 specific questions
  • Link each question to a specific objective
  • Ensure they are answerable through research
  • Check for interestingness and feasibility

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Learn from Others' Errors

Problem Statement Mistakes:

  • Too broad: "Social media is bad for society"
  • No evidence: Making claims without citations
  • Not real-world: Focusing only on academic gaps
  • Too long: Exceeding 300 words

Objective Mistakes:

  • Vague verbs: "To study..." instead of "To analyze..."
  • Too ambitious: Objectives that are impossible to achieve
  • Not measurable: Can't determine if achieved
  • Misaligned: Don't address the problem statement

Research Question Mistakes:

  • Yes/No questions: Not complex enough for research
  • Too many variables: Trying to study everything at once
  • Unfeasible: Requiring resources you don't have
  • Already answered: Repeating existing research exactly
