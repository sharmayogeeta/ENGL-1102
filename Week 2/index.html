Research Hypothesis & SMART Goals

What is a Research Hypothesis?

A research hypothesis is a clear, testable prediction about the relationship between variables in your study. It guides your research direction and methodology.

Finding & Evaluating Credible Sources

What Makes a Source Credible?

A credible source is free from bias and backed up with evidence. It is written by a trustworthy author or organization.

The CRAAP Test

  • Currency: Is the source up-to-date?
  • Relevance: Is the source relevant to your research?
  • Authority: Who is the author? Are they trustworthy?
  • Accuracy: Is the source supported by evidence?
  • Purpose: What was the motive behind publishing this source?

Types of Sources

  • Primary: First-hand evidence (interviews, statistics, original research)
  • Secondary: Analysis of primary sources (journal articles, books)
  • Tertiary: Consolidation of sources (encyclopedias, dictionaries)

Where to Find Credible Sources

Academic Databases

  • JSTOR - Academic journals and books
  • Google Scholar - Academic search engine
  • PubMed - Medical and life sciences
  • Project Muse - Humanities and social sciences

Evaluating Web Sources

Trusted Domain Extensions:

  • .edu - Educational institutions
  • .gov - Government websites
  • .org - Non-profit organizations

Red Flags to Avoid

  • Vague terms and buzzwords
  • Emotional or subjective language
  • No author or publication date
  • Claims without evidence

Essential tools to structure and convey ideas effectively for specific purposes and audiences.

Argumentative

Persuade through logic and evidence

Persuasive

Influence emotions and beliefs

Expository

Explain and inform objectively

Narrative

Tell stories and present events

Descriptive

Create vivid imagery with details

üí° Key Point

Research writing often combines multiple rhetorical modes to achieve clarity, persuasion, and engagement.

Argumentative Mode

Purpose

Present a well-reasoned case supported by evidence to convince readers through logical reasoning.

Key Features

  • Claims supported by logic, evidence, and analysis
  • Use of credible sources to strengthen arguments
  • Counterarguments addressed to reinforce stance
  • Clear thesis statement

Applications in Research

  • Position papers and critical analyses
  • Research-based arguments in essays and articles

Example

Topic: "Renewable energy policies are essential for mitigating climate change"

Approach: Present thesis, support with emission reduction data, address economic concerns

Persuasive Mode

Purpose

Convince readers to adopt a particular viewpoint by appealing to emotions, values, and ethical considerations alongside logical reasoning.

Key Features

  • Emotional and ethical appeals (pathos and ethos)
  • Strong, convincing language
  • Attention to audience's beliefs and values
  • Combination of logic and emotion

Applications in Research

  • Persuasive essays and advocacy papers
  • Grant applications and proposals
  • Opinion pieces

Example

Topic: Mandatory vaccinations in schools

Approach: Combine emotional appeals (family stories) with factual evidence and ethical arguments about protecting vulnerable populations

Expository, Narrative & Descriptive Modes

Expository Mode

Purpose: Explain or inform without personal opinions

  • Clear, objective presentation
  • Logical organization with headings
  • Examples, definitions, and data

Applications: Literature reviews, research reports, instructional manuals

Narrative Mode

Purpose: Tell stories or present events chronologically

  • Storytelling structure
  • Anecdotes and case studies
  • Vivid descriptions

Applications: Case studies, ethnographic reports, reflective essays

Descriptive Mode

Purpose: Create vivid imagery using sensory details

  • Sensory details (sight, sound, touch, taste, smell)
  • Figurative language and imagery
  • Precise descriptions

Applications: Experimental descriptions, environmental settings

Effective Paragraph Structure

Essential Components

1. Thesis Statement

Clear, arguable claim that guides your entire essay

2. Topic Sentences

First sentence of each paragraph that introduces the main idea

3. Supporting Statements

Evidence, examples, and analysis that support your topic sentence

4. Conclusion

Summarizes main points and reinforces thesis without repetition

Paragraph Flow

  • Unity: All sentences relate to one main idea
  • Coherence: Ideas flow logically with transitions
  • Development: Adequate support and explanation

Requirements

  • Due: Initial post by Sunday, replies by Wednesday
  • Citations: Use APA format for all sources
